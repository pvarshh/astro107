The Accretion Disc

Figure 4: A simulation of a black hole's accretion disc. Note how the left side is brighter than the right due to Doppler Beaming (NASA/Goddard/Jeremy Schnittman).

Surrounding the black hole, you will likely see a swirling disk of gas and dust falling in. This is the Accretion Disc.

Friction within the disc heats the material to millions of degrees, causing it to glow brightly in X-rays and other wavelengths. It's a beautiful, deadly ring of fire.

Pro Tip: You will notice that one side of the disc appears brighter and bluer than the other. This is due to Doppler Beaming. The material moving towards you is brighter (blueshifted), while the material moving away is dimmer (redshifted).

Gravitational Lensing

Figure 5: How gravity bends light to create the "halo" or Einstein Ring (NASA/Goddard).

Black holes bend light so intensely that they act like a giant lens. This phenomenon is called Gravitational Lensing.

Photo Op: Look closely at the edge of the black hole's shadow. You might see a distorted image of the stars behind the black hole, or even the back of the accretion disc bent over the top!

Because gravity bends light, you can theoretically see the back of your own head if you orbit at the Photon Sphere ($1.5 \times R_s$). Light orbits the black hole in a circle here, meaning if you look straight ahead, you see your own back!

The Shadow

The dark area in the center is not just the event horizon itself, but the Shadow of the black hole. Because gravity bends light inward, the shadow appears about 2.6 times larger than the actual event horizon.
